My Top Three Personality Traits

★ Curiosity

From a young age I have always wanted to know how things work. Whether dismantling old electronics or exploring the logic behind a medical condition, curiosity is the engine behind everything I do. In my academic life, this trait pushes me to go beyond lecture notes and explore supplementary research, tutorials, and experiments on my own. It is the reason I find subjects like data structures, machine learning, and anatomy equally exciting. Curiosity transforms studying from an obligation into an adventure, and it consistently drives me to achieve higher grades by seeking deeper understanding rather than surface-level memorisation.

★ Empathy

I genuinely care about people and their experiences. Empathy shapes the way I approach group projects, I listen carefully to teammates, consider different perspectives, and ensure everyone feels heard. In the context of technology, empathy is what keeps me grounded. It reminds me that every system I build will ultimately affect real human lives, especially in healthcare settings. This trait motivates me to write cleaner, more accessible code, and to think carefully about the ethical implications of any AI system I might one day design.

★ Perseverance

I do not give up easily. Debugging a programme for three hours, rewriting an essay four times, or studying a difficult concept until it clicks, perseverance is the trait that keeps me at the desk when everything else says to quit. Academically, this manifests as resilience in the face of challenging modules. When I struggled with discrete mathematics in my first year, I did not drop the course. Instead, I sought additional resources, formed a study group, and pushed through. Perseverance is the bridge between where I am and where I want to be.

My Hero: Senku Ishigami

“Get excited!”, Senku Ishigami, Dr. Stone

Background

Senku Ishigami is the protagonist of the manga and anime series Dr. Stone, created by Riichiro Inagaki and illustrated by Boichi. Set in a future where all of humanity has been petrified by a mysterious phenomenon, Senku, a teenage scientific genius, is one of the first people to break free. Rather than despair, he sets himself an audacious goal: use science to rebuild human civilisation from scratch, all the way from stone tools to smartphones, antibiotics, and rockets.

Why Senku Is My Hero

What makes Senku special is not just his encyclopaedic knowledge of science; it is his absolute belief that science can solve any problem. He faces every obstacle, disease, war, resource scarcity, and human ignorance, with an almost reckless optimism grounded in evidence and experiment. He embodies the idea that knowledge, systematically applied, is the most powerful tool in existence.

This resonates with me on a personal level because I hold the same conviction about technology and medicine. Just as Senku synthesises sulfa drugs to save a village from bacterial infection, I dream of building AI diagnostic tools that bring specialist-level healthcare to rural Jamaica. His catchphrase, “Get excited!”, is something I genuinely live by. Every time a piece of code compiles or a concept suddenly makes sense, I feel that same electric enthusiasm that drives Senku through every impossible challenge.

Senku also shows me that passion must be paired with discipline and curiosity. He does not just dream; he experiments, fails, analyses, and tries again. That scientific mindset, rigorous yet joyful, is the model I want for my own career in technology and healthcare innovation.

AI Businesses of 2025: AI-Powered Healthcare Diagnostics

Overview

The concept of AI-powered healthcare diagnostics ranks among the most transformative AI business ideas of 2025. This innovation reframes healthcare as a proactive, predictive system rather than a reactive one. AI applications can process and analyse data from wearables, medical imaging, and laboratory results, identifying the early stages of disease with remarkable precision. The blending of data analysis and automation gives healthcare providers the power to diagnose sooner, treat faster, and ultimately improve patient outcomes at scale.

Application in My World

With a background in technology and a deep desire to help others, I am convinced that Jamaica and the broader Caribbean region stand to benefit enormously from this innovation. Many communities, particularly in rural parishes, do not have access to specialist diagnostic equipment or experienced physicians. AI-based diagnostic tools could bridge that gap, enabling general practitioners to leverage machine-level accuracy for complex conditions. Additionally, patients with chronic diseases could be monitored continuously through wearable devices, reducing hospital admissions and making healthcare both more affordable and more accessible.

Ethical Concerns and My Solutions

This technology brings significant ethical challenges that must not be ignored:

  • Patient data privacy: Medical records are deeply personal. Breaches could expose sensitive information and erode trust in digital health systems.
  • Algorithmic bias: If training data does not represent diverse populations, AI models may produce less accurate diagnoses for certain ethnic or demographic groups.
  • Over-reliance on machines: Completely replacing human clinical judgement with AI outputs poses serious risks when systems encounter edge cases or novel presentations.

My proposed solutions include mandating end-to-end encryption and strict data governance frameworks for all health AI platforms; requiring diverse, representative datasets in model training; and positioning AI strictly as a clinical decision-support tool rather than a replacement for doctors. The human qualities of compassion, context-awareness, and ethical responsibility must always remain at the heart of medicine.

Real-World Example

One leading company in this space is PathAI (www.pathai.com), which uses artificial intelligence to assist pathologists in diagnosing diseases from tissue samples. Their platform increases diagnostic accuracy, reduces human error, and accelerates the time from biopsy to diagnosis, demonstrating that AI, when deployed responsibly, can be a genuinely life-saving ally in modern medicine.

Rohn, J., & Widener, C. (2005). Twelve Pillars. Jim Rohn International.

Chapter 4 - Achieve Your Goals

“The major reason for setting a goal is for what it makes of you to accomplish it.”

Chapter 4 follows Michael and his mentor Charlie as they discuss the power of goal-setting. The central lesson is disarmingly simple: write your goals down. Charlie explains that the act of writing a goal transforms a vague wish into a cognitive reality, once your mind knows exactly what you want, it begins working to make it happen. He encourages Michael to list one hundred things he wants to accomplish before he dies, not as an overwhelming exercise, but as a clarifying, motivating ritual.

The deeper insight in this chapter is that the true value of a goal is not the destination but the person you become in pursuing it. As Charlie puts it, quoting Mr Davis: “Make a million dollars, not for the million dollars, but for what you will have to become to make a million dollars.” Goals are therefore tools of personal transformation, not just checkboxes.

How this applies to my life: I have always been a dreamer but not always a planner. Reading this chapter reminded me that dreaming without writing is just daydreaming. I have since started keeping a goals journal where I record my ambitions, from completing my degree with distinction to one day building a health-tech startup in Jamaica. The chapter also shifted my perspective on setbacks: every challenge I face while working toward a goal is shaping me into someone capable of achieving the next, harder goal. That mindset is profoundly liberating.

Chapter 5 - The Proper Use of Time

“Every day has many opportunities, but only one best opportunity.”

In this chapter, Michael confesses that he feels he has no time to pursue his goals. Charlie’s response is memorable: “We must all suffer from one of two pains: the pain of discipline or the pain of regret. The difference is discipline weighs ounces while regret weighs tons.” The chapter argues that most people fall into a “time management trap”, they spend their days responding to what is urgent rather than focusing on what is truly important. Urgency is a tyrant, and unless you consciously design your schedule around your goals, time will always belong to someone else’s agenda.

Charlie uses the powerful image of a deathbed to drive the point home: at the end of your life, the weight of regret for things undone is far heavier than the temporary discomfort of discipline applied today. The chapter’s core principle is that you must be the designer of your life. If you don’t build your own plan, you will spend your days executing someone else’s.

How this applies to my life: As a university student juggling coursework, personal projects, and family responsibilities, I relate deeply to Michael’s sense of being overwhelmed. This chapter challenged me to honestly evaluate how I spend each hour. I began time-blocking my study sessions and protecting at least one hour each day for work that aligns with my long-term vision, whether that is practising Python, researching AI in medicine, or working on personal projects like this portfolio. The concept of the “one best opportunity” each day has become a daily compass for me.

Chapter 6 - Surround Yourself with the Best People

“Don’t join an easy crowd; you won’t grow. Go where the expectations and the demands to perform are high.”

Chapter 6 tackles one of the most uncomfortable truths in personal development: the people around you shape who you become. Charlie challenges Michael to examine his closest friendships and ask a simple but piercing set of questions: Who am I around? What effect are they having on me? What have they got me reading, saying, thinking, becoming? He introduces a three-category framework for all associations: disassociation, limited association, and expanded association. Each relationship should be evaluated by whether it is moving you in the direction of your goals or away from them.

Crucially, Charlie does not advocate for ruthless social pruning. He acknowledges the emotional difficulty of changing long-standing relationships. Instead, he frames it as a journey: not everyone who starts the trip with you is meant to go the full distance. Successful people actively seek out other successful people who challenge and inspire them, because, as Charlie quotes Mr Davis: “To attract attractive people you must be attractive.” The work begins with developing yourself.

How this applies to my life: This chapter made me reflect carefully on the communities I belong to. I have realised the immense value of my Computer Science cohort at UTech, being surrounded by peers who are equally ambitious and curious keeps my standard high. It also inspired me to seek out online communities of developers, health-tech innovators, and Caribbean entrepreneurs. I cannot always choose my immediate environment, but I can deliberately seek mentors, study partners, and role models who operate at the level I aspire to reach.

Chapter 7 - Be a Life-Long Learner

“Formal education will make you a living. Self-education will make you a fortune.”

The final chapter covered in this section is perhaps the most immediately applicable to any student. Charlie argues that far too many people treat graduation as the finish line of learning, when in reality it is barely the starting gun. Most of life, its challenges, opportunities, relationships, and demands, unfolds after formal education ends. The people who thrive are those who commit to learning for the rest of their lives through three key practices: reading books, observing and learning from successful people, and reflecting on their own experiences.

When Michael dismisses books as unnecessary, Charlie’s response is sharp: “It isn’t what the book costs. It’s what it will cost you if you don’t read it.” Books are described as the closest thing to having dinner with the greatest minds in history. And self-education is positioned not as optional enrichment, but as the very engine of sustained success.

How this applies to my life: I am a student now, and it would be easy to assume that my degree is the summit of my learning journey. This chapter firmly dismantled that assumption. My formal education in Computer Science is a foundation, not a ceiling. I have committed to reading at least one non-textbook per month, currently working through The Pragmatic Programmer alongside fiction and personal development titles. I also treat every internship, project, and conversation with a more experienced developer as a classroom. Learning never stops, and this chapter reminded me that the moment I think I know enough is the moment I start falling behind.
